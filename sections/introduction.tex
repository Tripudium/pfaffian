\section{Introduction}
The problem of bounding the volume of a tubular neighbourhood of a set in terms of its geometric complexity has a long and rich history. In 1840, Steiner~\cite{steiner1840bestimmung} observed that the volume of the $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood of a convex body in $\R^3$ is a quadratic polynomial in $\varepsilon$. A celebrated generalisation is due to Weyl~\cite{weyl1939volume}, who showed that the volume of the $\varepsilon$-tubular neighbourhood of a compact Riemannian submanifold of $\R^n$ is a polynomial in $\varepsilon$ whose coefficients are intrinsic curvature invariants; see also Hotelling~\cite{hotelling1939tubes} and the monograph by Gray~\cite{gray2004tubes}. Tube formulae came into the radar of numerical analysis through the work of Smale, Kostlan, Renegar, and Demmel, among others, who were interested in the probabilistic analysis of condition numbers~\cite{burgisser2013condition}. The key observation is that if the set of ill-posed inputs of a numerical problem can be described as a subset of an algebraic variety, then a bound on the volume of its tubular neighbourhood directly translates into a bound on the probability distribution of the condition number.

In~\cite{lotz2015volume}, the problem of bounding the volume of a tubular neighbourhood was studied for the zero set of a system of polynomial equations, where the bound is given in terms of the degrees of the defining polynomials. The approach combines Weyl's tube formula with integral geometry and degree bounds on the generalised Gauss map, and the resulting bounds are polynomial in the ratio $\varepsilon/\sigma$ (where $\sigma$ is the radius of the ambient ball) with coefficients determined by the degrees. These results have been extended to singular algebraic sets by Basu and Lerario~\cite{basu2021hausdorff}.

Our main result generalises these tubular neighbourhood bounds from the algebraic setting to the case of sets defined by Pfaffian functions. Pfaffian functions, introduced by Khovanskii~\cite{khovanskiui1991fewnomials}, are real analytic functions that satisfy triangular systems of first-order partial differential equations with polynomial coefficients. They strictly generalise polynomials, while retaining sufficient algebraic structure for effective quantitative bounds. The key tool is Khovanskii's theorem, which bounds the number of solutions of a system of Pfaffian equations in terms of a triple $(\alpha, \beta, s)$ called the Pfaffian \emph{format}. The sets defined by Pfaffian functions are tame in the sense of o-minimal geometry~\cite{wilkie1996model, wilkie1999theorem}, and many of their geometric and topological properties, such as the number of connected components and the sum of Betti numbers, can be bounded effectively in terms of the format~\cite{gabrielov2004complexity}.

More precisely, if $V = \mathcal{Z}(f)$ is a bounded, smooth hypersurface defined by a Pfaffian function $f$ with format $(\alpha, \beta, s)$, $\beta\geq 2$, and $X$ is uniformly distributed in a ball $B(p,\sigma)$, we show that (Theorem~\ref{thm:prob_bound})
\begin{equation*}
\prob\{ d(X, V) \le \varepsilon \} \leq C_{\alpha,\beta,s,n}\left(1+(\alpha+\beta+1)\frac{\varepsilon}{\sigma}\right)^n,
\end{equation*}
where 
$C_{\alpha,\beta,s,n}$ is a constant depending on the format.

The bound is derived by combining a variant of Weyl's tube formula with degree bounds on the generalised Gauss map. When specialised to the algebraic case ($s = 0$), the bound reduces to one of the same form as in~\cite{lotz2015volume}, with the degrees of the defining polynomials playing the role of $\beta$.

A prominent class of Pfaffian functions arises from neural networks with smooth activation functions. The logistic sigmoid $\sigma(x)=(1+\mathrm{e}^{-x})^{-1}$, $\tanh$, the softplus, and many other common activations are Pfaffian (see Table~\ref{tab:activations}), and a fully connected neural network composed of such activations is itself a Pfaffian function whose format can be expressed in terms of the network depth~$\ell$, width~$h$, and the format of the activation function (Proposition~\ref{prop:nn-format}). The decision boundaries of neural network classifiers with Pfaffian activations are therefore Pfaffian sets, and our tubular neighbourhood bounds apply.

Neural networks have been studied in the context of Pfaffian functions since the work of Macintyre and Sontag~\cite{macintyre1993finiteness}, who established finiteness results for sigmoidal networks. Karpinski and Macintyre~\cite{karpinski1997polynomial} showed that the VC dimension of sigmoidal Pfaffian networks is polynomial in the number of parameters; this was recently extended by D'Inverno, Bianchini, and Scarselli~\cite{dinverno2024vc} to graph neural networks with general Pfaffian activations. Bianchini and Scarselli~\cite{bianchini2014complexity} studied the topological complexity (sum of Betti numbers) of the decision regions of networks with Pfaffian activation functions, establishing that deep networks can produce exponentially more complex decision boundaries than shallow ones.

The robustness of neural network classifiers with respect to adversarial perturbations has been of practical concern in contexts ranging from spam filtering to medical diagnosis, and a systematic investigation was initiated by Szegedy et al.~\cite{szegedy2013intriguing}. A classifier $F\colon \R^n\to \R^m$ partitions the input space into decision regions separated by a decision boundary $\Sigma$. The distance $d(x,\Sigma)$ is the minimal perturbation needed to change the classification of an input~$x$, and one can define a condition number $\mC(x) = \|x\|/d(x,\Sigma)$ by analogy with the theory of conditioning in numerical analysis~\cite{burgisser2013condition}. Bounding the volume of the tubular neighbourhood of $\Sigma$ therefore provides quantitative robustness guarantees and, more precisely, tail bounds on the probability distribution of the condition number.

For a neural network classifier with $\ell$ hidden layers, $h$ total hidden units, and Pfaffian activations, applying the Gaussian variant of our tube formula to the pairwise decision boundaries yields (Theorem~\ref{thm:condition-gaussian})
\begin{equation*}
  \Prob(\mC_{\overline{x}}(X) > t) \;\leq\; C_{h,n,m}\cdot\frac{n(2\ell+2)}{t} + O(1/t^2),
\end{equation*}
where $X\sim\mathcal{N}(\overline{x}, \sigma^2\mathrm{Id})$ and $\mC_p(X) = \|X-p\|/d(X,\Sigma)$ is the local condition number. The bound decays as $O(1/t)$ for large $t$, matching the generic behaviour of condition-number tails for smooth hypersurfaces; the constant $C_{h,n,m}$, however, carries an exponential Khovanski\u\i{} factor $2^{h(h-1)/2}$ arising from the standard Pfaffian intersection bound in which chain elements are peeled off one at a time. For single-hidden-layer sigmoid networks with rational weights in general position, a direct argument based on the Bernstein--Kushnirenko--Khovanski\u\i{} theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:prob_bound_single_layer}, Corollary~\ref{cor:nn-condition-single-layer}) eliminates this exponential factor, replacing $C_{h,n,m}$ by a polynomial $C_n(L)\,w^n$ in the width $w$, with $L$ the lattice constant of the weights. A matching lower bound $\Omega(w^n)$ is achieved by an explicit grid construction (Remark~\ref{rem:single-layer-lowerbound}), showing that the exponent $n$ per layer-width factor is sharp. Whether an analogous polynomial-in-widths bound holds for deeper sigmoid networks is the content of Conjecture~\ref{conj:multi-layer-degree}, motivated by the corresponding linear-region bounds for piecewise-linear networks~\cite{montufar2014,hanin2019}.

\subsection{Notation and conventions} We denote by $\N$ the set of natural numbers including $0$, and by $\N_{>0}$ the set of positive natural numbers. For $n\in \N$, we use the notation $[n] := \{k\in \N_{>0}\colon k\leq n\}$. For $d,n\in \N$, we denote by $\R[X_1, \ldots, X_n]_{(\le d)}$ the vector subspace of the polynomial ring $\R[X_1,\dots,X_n]$ containing polynomials of degree at most $d$. We often implicitly identify a polynomial $P$ with the function $P\colon \R^n\to \R$ given by $P(x)=P(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ for $x\in \R^n$.
